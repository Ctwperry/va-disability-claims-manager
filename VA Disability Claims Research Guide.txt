The Comprehensive Jurisprudence of United States Military Disability: A Strategic Analysis of the Veterans Affairs Schedule for Rating Disabilities (VASRD), Evidentiary Standards, and the PACT Act Regulatory Framework
The system of veterans' disability compensation in the United States is an intricate architecture of administrative law, clinical medicine, and statutory entitlement designed to compensate for the average impairment of earning capacity resulting from service-connected injuries or diseases.1 Governed primarily by the Department of Veterans Affairs (VA) under the authority of Title 38 of the Code of Federal Regulations, specifically Part 4, the program seeks to fulfill the social contract between the state and those who have served in the armed forces.2 The transition from active duty to veteran status involves a complex adjudication process that requires the synthesis of military service records, clinical diagnoses, and specialized medical opinions.4
Central to this system is the Veterans Affairs Schedule for Rating Disabilities (VASRD), which serves as the guide for evaluating the severity of disabilities resulting from all types of diseases and injuries encountered as a result of, or incident to, military service.1 The degree of severity is expressed as a percentage rating, ranging from 0 to 100 percent in increments of 10, which directly correlates to the amount of monthly financial compensation provided to the veteran.1 Following the National Defense Authorization Act of 2008, the military branches were required to utilize the VASRD to evaluate injured soldiers, aligning the Department of Defense (DoD) and VA systems, although the DoD only rates conditions that render a service member "unfit" for duty, whereas the VA rates all conditions deemed service-connected.1
The Regulatory Framework of the VASRD Body Systems
The VASRD is organized into subparts that address specific physiological and psychological systems. Each system contains unique diagnostic codes (DCs) that provide the criteria for specific disability ratings.2 Adjudicators use these codes in conjunction with the M21-1 Adjudication Procedures Manual to determine the evaluation to be awarded.6
The Musculoskeletal and Muscle Systems
The musculoskeletal system is arguably the most frequently utilized section of the VASRD, covering the bones, joints, and spine.2 Ratings in this category are primarily determined by "functional loss" and the limitation of "range of motion" (ROM).2 Adjudicators look for evidence of pain, weakness, or fatigue that interferes with the normal functioning of a joint.2


VASRD Musculoskeletal Category
	Common Conditions and Diagnostic Basis
	Spine (Cervical and Thoracolumbar)
	Rated based on the degree of forward flexion. For example, forward flexion of the thoracolumbar spine of 30 degrees or less results in a 40% rating.7
	Major Joints (Knee, Hip, Shoulder)
	Evaluated primarily through ROM measurements. The "painful motion" rule (38 CFR § 4.59) ensures a minimum 10% rating if pain is present during movement, even if ROM is otherwise normal.2
	Muscle Injuries
	Rated based on the severity of the loss of muscle tissue or strength, often using a "slight," "moderate," "moderately severe," or "severe" scale.2
	Foot Conditions
	Includes flatfoot (pes planus) and plantar fasciitis, often rated based on whether the deformity is unilateral or bilateral and the presence of pain.2
	The "bilateral factor" is a critical nuance in musculoskeletal ratings. When a disability affects both sides of the body (e.g., both legs or both arms), the VA adds 10% of the combined rating for those specific items to the overall total to account for the heightened impairment of bilateral dysfunction.2
Sensory Organs: Vision and Audition
The organs of special sense, including sight and hearing, are governed by rigorous objective testing requirements. Vision is evaluated based on central visual acuity, contraction of the visual field, and muscle function.2 For hearing loss, the VA requires a specific audiometric test conducted by a licensed audiologist, measuring both speech discrimination and pure-tone thresholds.8
Tinnitus remains the most common claim in the VA system.8 It is currently assigned a flat 10% rating under Diagnostic Code 6260, regardless of whether it manifests in one ear or both.7 However, proposed regulatory changes seek to move tinnitus from a standalone rating to a symptom of underlying hearing loss, which could fundamentally alter the compensation landscape for future claimants.10
Respiratory and Cardiovascular Systems
The respiratory system covers conditions such as asthma, COPD, and sleep apnea.7 Sleep apnea is frequently rated at 50% if the veteran is prescribed a continuous positive airway pressure (CPAP) machine.7 The cardiovascular system focuses on heart disease and hypertension.2 Hypertension, long a common claim, was recently added as a presumptive condition for Agent Orange exposure, significantly expanding eligibility for Vietnam-era veterans.12
The Three Pillars of Service Connection: The Caluza Triangle
A successful disability claim requires more than the existence of a medical condition; it necessitates proof of "service connection".4 This is established through three essential elements, often referred to by practitioners as the "Caluza Triangle".5
1. A Current Diagnosis: The veteran must have a formal diagnosis of a physical or mental disability from a qualified medical professional.4 While chronic pain can be compensated without a specific name if it causes functional impairment, a formal diagnosis remains the gold standard for adjudication.15
2. An In-Service Event, Injury, or Disease: There must be documented evidence of an event that occurred during active duty that could have caused the condition.4 This includes traumatic injuries, exposure to environmental toxins, or the onset of a chronic disease.5
3. A Medical Nexus: This is the critical link connecting the current diagnosis to the in-service event.5 This link is typically established through a "Nexus Letter"—a formal opinion from a healthcare provider stating that it is "at least as likely as not" (a 50% probability or greater) that the condition was caused or aggravated by military service.16
Second-Order Insights: Aggravation and Continuity
The VA acknowledges that military service may not always "cause" a condition but may "aggravate" a pre-existing one.2 In such cases, the veteran must provide evidence that the condition worsened beyond its natural progression during service.5 Furthermore, for claims filed years after discharge, establishing "continuity of symptomatology" is vital. This requires medical records showing that the veteran sought treatment or experienced symptoms consistently since leaving the military, bridging the temporal gap that often leads to denials based on intervening causes.5
The Presumptive Doctrine and the Impact of the PACT Act
The presumptive doctrine is a legal mechanism that waives the requirement for a medical nexus for certain conditions based on a veteran's service location or era.19 If a veteran served in a designated location and develops a condition on the presumptive list, the VA "presumes" the condition is service-connected.12
The PACT Act and Toxic Exposures
The Sergeant First Class Heath Robinson Honoring Our Promise to Address Comprehensive Toxics (PACT) Act of 2022 expanded these presumptions to include veterans exposed to burn pits and other environmental hazards in the post-9/11 and Gulf War eras.12

Gulf War Illness and Infectious Diseases
Gulf War veterans who served in the Southwest Asia theater are eligible for presumptions related to "Medically Unexplained Chronic Multi-Symptom Illnesses" (MUCMIs).21 This includes Chronic Fatigue Syndrome, Fibromyalgia, and functional gastrointestinal disorders like Irritable Bowel Syndrome (IBS).12 Additionally, certain infectious diseases are presumptive if they manifest within one year of separation, including Malaria, West Nile Virus, and Q Fever.12
Specialized Claims: Mental Health and Individual Unemployability
Mental health claims represent a significant portion of VA adjudications and are rated under a general formula that focuses on "occupational and social impairment".2 Ratings are typically 0, 10, 30, 50, 70, or 100 percent.22
Post-Traumatic Stress Disorder (PTSD)
PTSD claims have specific evidentiary requirements, primarily the "stressor" requirement.22 The veteran must prove that they experienced a traumatic event in service, which is often corroborated by "markers" in personnel records (e.g., sudden performance drops) or buddy statements for non-combat stressors such as personal trauma or military sexual trauma (MST).15
The VA is currently transitioning to a domain-based rating system for all mental health conditions, scoring five specific areas of functioning:
1. Cognition (understanding and communicating).10
2. Interpersonal interactions and relationships.10
3. Task completion and life activities.10
4. Self-care and independent living.10
5. Social and occupational adaptability.10
Total Disability Based on Individual Unemployability (TDIU)
TDIU is a critical safety net for veterans whose service-connected disabilities are not rated at 100% but who are nonetheless unable to maintain "substantially gainful employment".23 This allows the VA to pay the veteran at the 100% disability rate.23

The Evidentiary Dossier: Essential Documentation and Forms
The success of a claim hinges on the quality of the "evidentiary record".5 The VA has a legal "Duty to Assist" veterans in gathering federal records, but the burden remains on the claimant to provide private records and identifying information.28
Administrative and Service Records
Every claim must be supported by the DD Form 214 (Report of Separation) to verify the dates and character of service.4 For active duty members filing before separation, the Benefits Delivery at Discharge (BDD) program allows filing between 180 and 90 days prior to discharge, requiring a completed Separation Health Assessment - Part A Self-Assessment and current service treatment records.4
Medical Evidence and Disability Benefits Questionnaires (DBQs)
Clinical evidence includes Service Treatment Records (STRs), VA medical records, and private treatment notes.5 Disability Benefits Questionnaires (DBQs) are standardized forms designed to help private physicians document the specific medical findings the VA requires to assign a rating according to the VASRD.9 While the VA provides no-cost exams, many veterans choose to have their own providers complete DBQs to ensure their symptoms are fully captured.9
Lay Evidence and the Power of Buddy Statements
Lay evidence consists of written testimony from the veteran or people who know them.4 These "buddy statements" are particularly useful when medical records are missing or when documenting the daily impact of a condition that clinicians might not witness.14
* VA Form 21-10210 (Lay or Witness Statement): This is the formal form for third-party testimony.4
* VA Form 21-4138 (Statement in Support of Claim): This allows the veteran to provide a narrative of their symptoms and the in-service events that caused them.4
The Adjudication Life Cycle: C&P Exams and Appeals
The adjudication process begins with the submission of VA Form 21-526EZ (Application for Disability Compensation).4 Once received, the VA often schedules a Compensation and Pension (C&P) Examination.9
The C&P Examination and the ACE Process
The C&P exam is the primary tool used by the VA to determine the severity of a disability and its service connection.9 The examiner, often a third-party contractor (e.g., VES, QTC, OptumServe), does not provide treatment but rather fills out a report for the VA adjudicator.9 If there is already sufficient medical evidence in the file, the VA may use the Acceptable Clinical Evidence (ACE) process, where a provider reviews the records and provides an opinion without an in-person meeting.9
Decision Review and the Appeals Modernization Act
If a claim is denied, the veteran has three primary options for appeal under the Appeals Modernization Act:
1. Higher-Level Review (HLR): A de novo review of the same evidence by a more senior adjudicator.29
2. Supplemental Claim: The submission of "new and relevant" evidence that was not considered in the previous decision.4
3. Board Appeal: Taking the case to a Veterans Law Judge at the Board of Veterans' Appeals (BVA), which allows for an evidence submission, a hearing, or a direct review.29
Strategic Support: VSOs and Regional Resources in Maryland
The complexity of the VA system often necessitates professional assistance. Veterans Service Officers (VSOs) are accredited representatives who provide free help with claims.31

Identifying and Mitigating Common Claim Denials
A significant portion of VA claims are initially denied due to administrative or evidentiary shortcomings.18 Identifying these failure points is essential for a successful first-time filing.
The Anatomy of a Denial


Common Reason for Denial
	Description and Mitigation Strategy
	Lack of Service Connection
	The VA finds no link between the condition and service. Mitigation: Obtain a strong medical Nexus Letter.15
	Insufficient Evidence
	Missing STRs or private records. Mitigation: Review the file for gaps and use the Freedom of Information Act to request C&P exam reports.9
	Negative C&P Exam
	The examiner's opinion is unfavorable. Mitigation: Provide a rebuttal letter or an Independent Medical Examination (IME) from a private specialist.5
	Incorrect Forms or Signatures
	Technical errors that stall the claim. Mitigation: Double-check all fields on the 21-526EZ and consult a VSO before submission.15
	Condition Not Severe Enough
	Service connection is granted but at a 0% rating. Mitigation: Provide evidence of functional loss, such as an inability to walk or work.35
	The Fully Developed Claim (FDC) Advantage
The Fully Developed Claim (FDC) program is the most efficient pathway for a veteran who has already gathered all their medical and lay evidence.36 By certifying that no further evidence exists, the veteran allows the VA to skip the evidence-gathering phase, potentially reducing the decision time from months to weeks.29 However, if the veteran submits additional evidence after filing the FDC, the claim is converted to a "Standard Claim," losing its priority status.29
Conclusions and Strategic Recommendations
The VA disability system is a complex but navigable ecosystem for the veteran who prioritizes objective medical evidence and professional administrative support. The passage of the PACT Act has fundamentally shifted the burden of proof for millions of veterans, making presumptive service connection the new baseline for many chronic and terminal conditions. However, for conditions not covered by presumptions—such as musculoskeletal injuries and secondary mental health disorders—the "Caluza Triangle" remains the ultimate legal standard.
Veterans should view their disability claim as a clinical and legal dossier that must be built chronologically and supported by the "at least as likely as not" standard of proof. By utilizing Disability Benefits Questionnaires (DBQs) and maintaining a continuous record of post-service treatment, claimants can effectively bridge the temporal gap that frequently leads to initial denials. Furthermore, the integration of lay evidence through "buddy statements" provides a human dimension to the medical record, documenting the functional limitations that are the core of the VASRD rating philosophy. For those in the Maryland and Baltimore regions, the proximity to the Regional Office and specialized VSO programs offers a significant strategic advantage in ensuring that claims are prepared, presented, and prosecuted to the highest standard of administrative law.
